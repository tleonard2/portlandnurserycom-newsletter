Take aim at Slugs!

It's a great time for Sluggo!

Sluggo kills slugs and snails and can safely be used around pets and wildlife! Contains iron phosphate, which occurs naturally in soil. Sluggo remains effective after rain.

SLUGGO will attact slugs and snails out of their hiding places. After eating the bait, slugs and snails will stop feeding, become less mobile and begin to die within three to six days.

This physiological effect gives immediate protection to plants even though the slugs and snails may remain in the area. Dead slugs and snails may not be visible because they often crawl off to a secluded place to die.

 

Weed focus!

Weeds are thriving!

This time of year teeny tiny weeds are popping up everywhere. Temperatures are mild, irrigation is plentiful and the soil is soft and warm which adds up to happy little weeds.  Instead of breaking your back trying to pluck every one, try the Winged Weeders. 

These innovatively shaped tools glide effortlessly through your garden. They work with a push and a pull to dislodge and scoop up weeds. The pointed tip makes it easy to move around your desirable plantings without incident.

We also carry the Winged Weeder Junior with a short handle for more precise, up-close weeding.

Building stunning containers!

Using Thrillers, Spillers and Fillers to build containers!

April is a good time to be planting your containers. We are updating our last year's feature on creating containers with "Thrillers, Spillers and Fillers" with new lists of the possible plantings for each.

Building containers with a combination of plants is a challenge and one that is a pleasure for every gardener. Use the simple formula of combining a 'thriller' with a 'spiller' and a 'filler.'

Start with a “thriller,” a centerpiece plant with star quality, something big, bold, and beautiful such as the coleus shown above. Surround it with a filler, for example, a fuchsia and then fill the remainder with a spiller such as this Sedum 'Ogon.'

Use each of those kinds of plants—in various proportions—and take care to balance colors and textures. We have selections of a rainbow of colors in each.

Tomato selection is ripe!

Tomatoes are the quintessential for the summer veggie garden for many people. Everyone’s passions come out when the tomatoes come in. It’s no wonder they were once called “love apples.”

Our tomato selection is ramping up here at Portland Nursery! We will be carrying over 300 varieties this year with our peak selection between the last week of April through mid to late May.

Planting tips

  • Tomatoes need at least six hours of direct sun.
  • Amend soil with compost or manure, and use an all-purpose fertilizer (such as Sure Start) and a handful of lime in the bottom of the hole.
  • Use Cozy Coats, Season Starters or a cold frame to protect your tomatoes when night temperatures are between 46º-55º to avoid stunting.
  • Red plastic tomato mulch can help warm the soil when the weather is cool, and retain moisture when it is hot.

Plant Picks of the Month

Plant Picks of the Month

PERENNIAL PICK - GEUM
Geum is a long blooming member of the rose family which will reward you with flowers in shades of yellow, orange, red, or pink. The flowers are bee magnets and work well as a cut flower. It is a low allergen plant. The foliage ranges from deciduous to evergreen depending on species and conditions. Read more...

 

TREE/SHRUB PICK - Deciduous Azaleas & Rhododendrons
Rhododendrons and Azaleas have long been the go-to plant for northwest gardens. They love it here, so it’s an easy choice. There is a large group of them however that has been overlooked for years; the ones that lose their leaves in winter. Dramatic flower color and sweet perfume, lovely fall leaves and self-sufficiency are some of their best qualities. Plus they thrive in sunshine while their evergreen relatives burn. Read more...

 

NATIVE PICK - CORYLUS CORNUTA – Beaked Hazelnut
We truly missed the presence and availability of all hazelnuts for the decade or so beginning in the mid-1990s, when the virulent Eastern Filbert Blight made a serious threat to the orchards in the Willamette Valley, and resulted in quarantine of all Corylus, preventing their transport and sale..
