Take aim at Slugs!

It's a great time for Sluggo!

Sluggo kills slugs and snails and can safely be used around pets and wildlife! Contains iron phosphate, which occurs naturally in soil. Sluggo remains effective after rain.

SLUGGO will attact slugs and snails out of their hiding places. After eating the bait, slugs and snails will stop feeding, become less mobile and begin to die within three to six days.

This physiological effect gives immediate protection to plants even though the slugs and snails may remain in the area. Dead slugs and snails may not be visible because they often crawl off to a secluded place to die.

 

Weed focus!

Weeds are thriving!

This time of year teeny tiny weeds are popping up everywhere. Temperatures are mild, irrigation is plentiful and the soil is soft and warm which adds up to happy little weeds.  Instead of breaking your back trying to pluck every one, try the Winged Weeders. 

These innovatively shaped tools glide effortlessly through your garden. They work with a push and a pull to dislodge and scoop up weeds. The pointed tip makes it easy to move around your desirable plantings without incident.

We also carry the Winged Weeder Junior with a short handle for more precise, up-close weeding.

Rhododendrons and Azaleas Galore!

Full stock of Rhododendrons and Azaleas

You can never have too many rhododendrons! Come see all the great stock we have in.

Pacific In the Northwest one of the most popular garden shrubs is the rhododendron. One of the greatest advantages of this shrub is that it is evergreen and many varieties flower at varying times of the year. In fact, by proper selection of varieties it's possible to have continual flowering for up to six months or more. In this article we’ll discuss a few of the early flowering varieties. At a time when there is not much flower color in the garden it is nice to be able to include plants that provide early color. The early flowering rhododendrons vary in color from beautiful pinks, rose, reds, yellows, whites, purples and lavenders.

Check out the Rhododendron Society website for culture information and photo features.

Plant Picks of the Month

Perennial Pick of the Month:

FRITILLARIA
The spring blooms of Frittilaria bring an exotic magic to the garden. It is as if the pendant bell shaped flowers are waiting to be worn by a fairy as a hat or skirt. A visit to Catherine Creek in Washington or Tom McCall nature preserve past Mosier, Oregon offer an opportunity to experience these magical flowers in the wild. Fritillaria have an incredible menagerie of colors. Read more...

 

PRUNUS: Cherries and their Prunus relatives
Prunus is a huge group of trees and shrubs that includes many of our favorite spring-flowering trees, some of the most common privacy hedges and arguably our tastiest fruits. Peaches, plums, nectarines, cherries and almonds are all different species of Prunus. Read more...

 

OEMLERIA: Indian Plum
So, is it Oemleria or Osmaronia? Best to be prepared to remember it by both names, for it is known by both names. More frequently Oemleria, as that is the original version; Osmaronia is apparently a “generic” name that has become more widespread. By whatever name, our native Indian Plum or Oso Berry is one of the heralds of spring in the woods west of the Cascades. Read more...
