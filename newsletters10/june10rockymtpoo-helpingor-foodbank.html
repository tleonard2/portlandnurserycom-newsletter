Rocky Point Poo

We like to carry local products that we find to be outstanding. Rocky Point Poo is one of those. Rocky Point Poo is a product of Rocky Point Stables in Scappoose, Oregon just north of Portland on Highway 30.

Rocky Point Poo compost is given the utmost care and attention to ensure you with a product perfect for your garden, plants, and flowers. The horse manure is cured, turned, and processed while monitoring the water and oxygen intake to stabilize the most nutrients as possible within the compost.

In Your Community: The Oregon Food Bank

Helping the Oregon Food Bank, Helping Feed the Community

The statistic is a little shocking: 240,000 people in Oregon ate from emergency food boxes in 2009, and of that number thirty-six percent were children.

If you're like me, you take access to good, healthy food a bit for granted. If, like me, you are able to visit a farmers market on a semi-regular basis and/or have vegetables growing in your garden as you read this, it's especially hard to imagine the scarcity of good food that many people face. Emergency food boxes are, understandably, filled primarily with non-perishable food, but that means very little fresh fruit and vegetables.

There are lots of people working to change that. Schools, correctional facilities, community garden sites are planting extra, or setting aside some of what they harvest, to take to local food pantries and relief agencies; the Multnomah County CROPS program is in its second year of supplying the Oregon Food Bank with thousands of pounds of fresh produce: Read about it at EcoMetro.

But the need still is greater than the supply.

And this is where you come in.

Do your neighbors close their curtains and turn off their lights when they see you walking down the street, bearing a teeming box or bag of surplus zucchini? Do you suddenly have more tomatoes than you know what to do with? Run out of ideas of what to do with all those green beans?

Whether you consciously planted extra or simply find you have more than you can use, it would be a welcome addition to emergency food boxes for people in your community.

And getting your extra produce to where it can be used is easy: visit the Oregon Food Bank website to see the list of the produce that works best for food boxes (hint: vegetables that don't have to be refrigerated), and maps where you can locate the food pantry closest to home.http://oregonfoodbank.org/Give-Food/Plant-a-Row-Program

Stop by Portland Nursery for more information

Stop in at the information desk at either Portland Nursery location and look at the Oregon Food Bank notebook, where you can see the neighborhood maps and find the closest food pantry, get a handout that gives the list of desired vegetables and other information. And as a thank you from us, take home a free packet of seeds!

As we come into the season of abundance, let's work together toward making it more abundant for everyone in our community "…because no one should be hungry." (OFB)

Plant Picks of the Month

PERENNIAL PICK - Impatiens
Impatiens is one of the best and brightest color accent for shady areas. Dead heading will help plants to continue to bloom all summer long. The first time you encounter the exploding seed pods of Impatiens you will never forget why they are called 'Touch-Me-Nots.' Read more...

TREE/SHRUB PICK - Jasminum
The common name Jasmine covers a broad array of vines and shrubs that come from all corners of the temperate world. Some of these are true Jasmines, from the Jasminum genus, but there many more Jasmines and Jessamines in different plant families that deserve attention. Read more...

NATIVE PICK - Sisyrinchium - Blue-eyed Grass
So, why don't I have Sisyrinchium in my garden? I should. And so should you. There's nothing not to love about it. Virtually maintenance-and-problem-free, the most beautiful shades of blue or purple or yellow you'll ever see. Depending on the species they want seasonably wet or good-drainage dry; in other words, there is a Sisyrinchium suitable for nearly any garden setting. Read more...
