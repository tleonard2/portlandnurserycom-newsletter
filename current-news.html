Trillium rivale >> 14.99 in a 2” pot.
Trillium Kurabyashi >> 19.99 in a 2” pot.
Trillium rivale is our smallest western trillium, growing about four to six inches high with white flowers usually speckled with dark spots in the throat; flowers may also have a pink tinge. The leaves may be green to silver-veined and occasionally a plant will appear to be coated with a light silver wash.
Seed matures more quickly to blooming size, in three to four years, and the plants can be divided easily. Rhizomes form fairly quickly and these offsets will be somewhat different in appearance compared to other trilliums.
It is suitable for troughs, pots and moist shady areas in the garden but keep it where you’ll see it easily. Trillium rivale grows well in full sun in nature and in gardens, as long as moisture is consistent. This trillium is usually the first to bloom.
Trillium kurabayashii is the most vigorous and reliable of the western trilliums and follows Trillium rivale closely in blooming sequence, sometimes ahead of or with Trillium ovatum. The leaves are mottled with a dark pattern which can vary in intensity depending on amount of shade, and the flowers vary from yellow to dark red. (The clone we received at Portland Nursery is red.)
It’s important to plant trilliums where they can remain undisturbed but if you must move them, dig deeply and include a large clump of soil because the rhizomes may be down twelve inches or more.
If you buy a pot of trilliums in leaf, you can sink the pot in the soil up to the pot rim and then transplant when dormant, in mid- to late fall depending on your weather and climate. Given the appropriate position and correct amount of moisture, trilliums are known to live for up to 50 years, and will gradually formed fair-sized clumps if left undisturbed.
Trilliums aren’t often susceptible to diseases but seem to be considered a good food source for voles, so if you have a problem with voles, planting them in wire baskets or large containers (no more than two-gallon) and lifting every two to three years may work better.
Deer do eat the flowers which can inhibit their growth and blooms for one or more years. Never pick a trillium and NEVER dig a trillium in the wild.
The Pacific Bulb Society Wiki has many excellent photos of trilliums, both our eastern and western species, showing the range of colors and giving more specific information for each one.
