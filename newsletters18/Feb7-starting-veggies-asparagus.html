Time to get those seeds a-popping!
Make your lists and come visit Portland Nursery to find the perfect seeds for your garden. Some seeds need to be started under grow-lights a full eight weeks before average last frost, which here in Portland means you’ll be revving up your seed-starting operation mid-February!
If you don’t know what varieties to grow we recommend heirloom varieties. Most of them originated before the 1950s and have been preserved and passed through multiple generations by seed saving. These were chosen because they “grow-true” and carry a remarkable trait, such as high yield, unique flavor, color or shape, exceptional disease resistance, heat tolerance, adaptability to soil types, etc.
If you want to learn more about heirloom vegetables check out our brochure. We recommend starting with seeding vegetables and herbs that do well indoors in early February such as:
1) Read instructions on back of selected packet to find the spacing, light and moisture requirements, as well as how long it will take for crop to grow.
2) Prepare tray or cell packs by filling with seedling soil mix. Place tray in standing water and let sit until the surface of the soil is moist. Take the container out of the water and allow it to drain thoroughly. Sow seeds thinly on to the surface by tapping them onto soil from a folded paper.
3) Lightly cover seeds with a fine layer of soil or vermiculite.
4) Place tray or cell pack in a sunny window or under a sun light. Cover with a piece of glass, plastic or plastic wrap over the top to maintain moisture. If necessary, cover with netting or newspaper to shade it from direct sun. When germination starts, remove both covers.
5) Continue to regularly water seedlings according to the back of the packet. When seedlings are large enough to handle, it’s time to either thin out the starts, or transplant them. If they were grown in cell packs, thin out by removing all but the strongest 1-3 starts in each cell. If grown in a tray, gently lift each seedling out of the soil - keeping as much of the roots intact as possible – and transplant each start into its own cell. Cover roots with soil.
6) Water in seedlings and use a waterproof stick tag and sharpie to label the plant's name, date of seed sowing and date of transplant.
For helpful hints planning your seed starting and garden refer to our Vegetable Planting Calendar. You can also find a wealth of information about growing food and individual crop care on our Vegetable page. Available at our stores is another invaluable and economical resource for seed starting and gardening, The Maritime Northwest Garden Guide, published by the Seattle Tilth.
We are delighted to answer all your seed starting questions, so please stop by either location, send us an email or give us a call.
Did you miss planting your fall bulbs?
We have blooming daffodils and hyacinths in pots!
Summer blooming bulbs are streaming into the store: fragrant lilies, enormous dahlias, regal gladiolus, hummingbird-attracting crocosmia, and the dramatic prehistoric foliage of elephant ears. You can begin planting bulbs in April through May when the danger of frost has passed.
Some bulbs such as peonies, glads and lilies can be planted now. Begonias can be planted in April. Plant Canna and Dahlia in May.
A long-lived perennial plant, asparagus makes delicious spears every spring with only a little maintenance; but it takes 2-3 years to come into full production. Asparagus is commonly grown from 2 year old crowns (roots), which may reach bearing size somewhat faster than seeds.
Caring for Asparagus: Asparagus yields best in full sun (at least six hours of direct sun), but tolerates light shade. An area sheltered from strong winds is preferred. Provide well-drained soil with a neutral pH. Plant asparagus roots in spring as soon as the soil is dry enough to dig. We have three varieties for you to bring home to your garden! Two are in now and the third, Jersey Knight, should be in by Valentine's Day.
Jersey Knight (should be in by Valentine's Day).
This all-male asparagus variety yields the highest quality spears of the Jersey hybrids. It has performed exceptionally well in trials all across the U.S.
  • Each mature plant produces 7-9 choice 7-10" spears tipped with a purplish blush. At that length, spear diameter is between 3/8-3/4".
  • Highly resistant to rust, fusarium, root rot, crown rot and cercospora.
  • Easily overwinters in the maritime Northwest.
Sweet Purple
This new type of asparagus has many similar characteristics to the green varieties but offers something new for the connoisseur. Crowns are both male and female.
  • Deep, burgundy spears.
  • 20% higher sugar content. Because of this extra sweetness, this vegetable is often eaten raw or as a salad garnish. When cooked, the sweetness gives the asparagus a mild, nutty flavor.
  • Spears are generally larger, more tender, and less stringy than their green counterpart. This allows the cook to use the entire spear with little waste.
Mary Washington
This female variety features long straight shoots which are very thick and heavy. The color is rich dark green and slightly tinged purple at the tightly folded tips.
  • Uniform, heavy yields, 60 day cutting season.
  • Holds a tight bud longer than most.
  • Somewhat rust resistant.
