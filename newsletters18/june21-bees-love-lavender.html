Lavender is cultivated worldwide for its volatile oils, fragrance, craft material, and is grown as a garden plant as far north as Norway. All 30 species of Lavender are small shrubs or herbs and are native to France and the western Mediterranean.
The basic requirements for successfully growing lavender are full sun and good drainage. Lavender is a hardy plant and will tolerate neglect, but will flourish best if these two requirements are met.
Due to poor drainage lavender plants may be subject to root rot. Additionally, windburn may affect some species. This will look similar to rust, but it is actually not fungal. The leaves will rejuvenate by the next spring.
Pruning is the best way to maintain a healthy, vigorous bush and should be done at least once a year for the whole life of the plant. Cut back one third of the bush in early to mid-autumn. Lavender flowers in spring and then again in late summer/autumn.
Lavenders generally transplant well but will usually not flower much during the first summer following transplanting. The best times to transplant are late autumn/early winter and very early spring.
Lavandula angustifolia: English Lavender is the most common narrow-leafed variety. It grows to about 1 to 3 feet high and occasionally taller in gardens. This lavender has short, but irregular, crooked stems that are covered with a flakey yellowish grey bark. The flowers grow on short stemmed laterals with one to six flowers on the terminal end.

Turn your garden into a haven for butterflies with native plants that welcome them to bed down and lay their eggs. When the caterpillars emerge from the eggs, breakfast is ready! Help butterflies of our region, Cascadia, flourish by planting host plants. They are larval-food!
You can target the species of butterfly you'd like to attract by filling your garden with the plants they love. Check out this article for Plant Information for Specific Butterfly Species.
Many butterflies are native to our region, and one way to attract them is by planting natives. Many of the NW Native plants we carry at the nursery are good butterfly plants as well as good garden plants. Here is a partial list of what we usually carry during the year.
  • Achillea millefolium: Yarrow
  • Allium cerneum: Wild Onion
  • Amelanchier alnafolia: Serviceberry
  • Aquilegia Formosa: Columbine
  • Asclepias speciosa: Showy Milkweed
  • Aster spp.: Aster
  • Camassia spp.: Camas
  • Dicentra formosa: Bleeding Heart
  • Erigeron spp: Flea Bane
  • Eriophyllum lanatum: Oregon sunshine
  • Holodiscus discolor: Oceanspray
  • Penstemon spp.: Penstemon
  • Rhamnus purshiana: Cascara
  • Philadelphus lewisii: Mock Orange
  • Sidalcea spp.: Checker Mallow
  • Spiraea spp.: Spirea
