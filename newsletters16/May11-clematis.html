Clematis from the Montana Group
String Clematis throughout your garden!
Clematis perk up any area of the garden. Plant around a lamppost, along a fence, in an arbor or latticework, on brush piles and mail boxes - just about any place that needs to be beautified!
Try growing one with a shrub or small tree so that when the clematis is in bloom the tree (or shrub) appears to be blooming. With over 200 species and a variety of growth habits, Clematis make versatile additions to any garden. Most have showy flowers, large in proportion to the size of the plant, that range in nearly every color from white, cream, yellow through pink, red, rich magenta into pal blues, lilac, and dark purple.
With its versatility and wide range of colors, you are sure to find one that fits perfectly into your landscape!
Suzy's favorites
Suzy Hancock, our in-store expert on Clematis
has picked out a few of her favorites:
  1. Any from the Viticella group especially 'Etoile Violette'
  2. 'Niobe' features a glowing red that can't be beat!
  3. The Montana group is the clematis version of dogwood flowers - they are big enough to grow through trees.
  4. 'Durandii,' a non-climbing type, is perfect for growing through shrubs and in pots.
  5. Fuji Musume' has about the prettiest shade of blue out there (and it is fun to say).

Who's bugging you?
Don't blame the NSA - these are some good bugs!
Many insects, fungi, and bacteria can benefit your garden in one way or another. Beneficials come in many shapes and sizes and each help your garden in their own way, including controlling pests and pollinating plants. Creating a suitable habitat in your garden will attract beneficials, helping you to have a sustainable garden and support native wildlife. When relying on beneficials as a form of pest control you must be patient and tolerant of a few pests in your garden, and some damage to your plants. Without a few pests around your beneficials won’t have anything to eat!
