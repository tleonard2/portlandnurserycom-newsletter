Agastache: Hyssop
Hot weather is coming!
Our selection of drought tolerant plants is extensive!
We had a nice refresher of rain this week but this weekend will be all about beating the heat. If you must plant, do it in the cooler parts of the day such as early morning or late evening. Whenever you are going to plant, start by standing your new plant in a bucket with a few inches of water so that the rootball can get fully hydrated (this is for plants in larger than a 4" pot). This can take an hour or more – so make sure the bucket is in the shade.
Dig a hole that is twice as big as you need for the size of the plant’s rootball. Half fill it with home-made compost mixed with some of the soil you have dug out. This will help to retain water around the roots. Once the rootball is hydrated and the hole is dug, fill the hole with water, let it drain and put the plant in the hole. Fill with more water, gently pushing the soil back into place around the plant.
When planting on a hot day, it is also a good idea to water in your new babies with Maxi Crop, or other product that can alleviate transplant shock. Keep new plants well watered for the next few weeks until they are established, but make sure you target your hose or watering can at their roots, rather than watering the leaves.
Better yet, wait to plant until the heat wave has past. Cooler weather is in the forecast for next week, so cool off with cobra misters or stay under an umbrella. Give your plants a break from the sun too. Throw a shade cloth over rhodies or anything that might need shade protection.

Coreopsis Beetle
We are having a difficult time obtaining coreopsis plants due to the arrival of a relatively new pest: coreopsis beetle. We will stock plants when we can. If you have questions about plant availability or beetle control, please stop by our Information Desk. Read on for more information and other resources below.
Two different beetles share top billing as posets of coreopsis plants Both are commonly called the coreopsis beetle, though they don’t look alike. Their fondness for devouring coreopsis leaves breaks the pest-resistant status that this plant species formerly enjoyed.
Both adults and larvae feed on the leaves, skeletonizing them. Plants can be completely stripped of their leaves. Control is a challenge. If you notice caterpillars in the spring when they begin feeding, Bacillus thuringiensis var. tenebrionis (Btt) is an organic microbiological insecticide that may kill the larvae. Adult beetles are most effectively controlled by handpicking and dropping them into soapy water to kill them.
