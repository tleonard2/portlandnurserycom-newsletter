Cityscaping with Laura Altvater
Today I’d like to share some of my observations as a botanist, farmer, herbalist and horticulturalist about the ways we enhance life in the garden. We can create healing habitats for people, as demonstrated by hospital gardens and horticultural therapy. The garden offers a delight for all the senses to help us relax. Of course, indoor habitat counts too. Tiny pots of succulents or fun terrariums can add a little spice of life to any indoor space. While, fostering our own well-being in our spaces large or small, we can also create habitat for wildlife.
Lightbulb Terrariums
Even a hummingbird needs a place to rest. If one has the means, it can be easily created no matter how big or small your space may be. A narrow tree in tiny garden or third story balcony paired with a hummingbird plant will give them a place to rest, ponder and get a bit of nectar (See our Plants for Hummingbirds brochure). Other wild birds such as goldfinches and robins may also visit your garden in search of seeds or worms. See our Attracting Birds to Your Garden brochure.
Swallowtail Butterfly on Agastache (Photo credit to Katie Gwyn).
Butterflies are called the floating flowers of the garden. It is easy to create an enticing place for them by placing a shallow dish of water with a stone for them to drink water. Swallow tails love Agastache and Red Admirals are attracted to Echinacea. Of course, the coveted Monarch prefer Asclepias species. (See our Attracting Butterflies brochure).
Frogs and bats add to some of the magical sounds and sights of a summer evening. Cool, moist shade and water are the basic components needed to encourage frogs in your garden. Bats help control many insects, such as mosquitoes, in the garden. They need an open area to fly and a cozy place to sleep. Cedar bat houses are a simple way to encourage bats to come to your aid.
Then there are the “critters” in the garden, like deer, raccoons, squirrels, coyotes and cats. Being a gardener means you create habitat for those furry, not always helpful, animals. Portland Nursery has some possible solutions for you such as repellants, scarecrow sprinklers and netting. A simple solution to keep cats out of new beds is to place sticks in the bare areas so they will not have a clear area to do their business. Deer can be a bit more difficult to control. Reapplication of repellants, fencing and using deer resistant plants are a few good strategies. (See our brochure on Deer Resistant plants). Remember, all deer are different and some trial and error with finding the right combination of plants will take some time.
Bee on Scabiosa (Photo credit to Laura Altvater).
The garden is teeming with activity this time of year. Bees are buzzing with their pollinator friends. Whether or not you “go native”, many plants can bring in the good guys in the bug world. Flat topped flowers such as Achillea, Eupatorium and Scabiosa attract a wide array of beneficial insects. Bees are drawn to a wide array of plants such as, Caryopteris, squash (did you know there are actual squash bees?), and sunflowers. The amazing relationship between insects and plants is at its climax this season. The “bad bugs” and disease are flourishing as well. Our information desk is a great place to bring your samples and photos so we can best help you find the solution. Gardening is not always straight forward. Unfortunately, some experiments need to happen before you find the right plant for the right place. Portland Nursery will do our best to help you with your gardening needs.
