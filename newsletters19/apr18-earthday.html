April 18, 2019
The amount of plastics in the ocean is growing. We're getting pretty good at bringing our own carry-out bags to the grocery - but what about all the plastic bags we grab for our veggies? Wouldn't it be great to step out on your deck or into your backyard and pick just enough lettuce for your dinner salad?
Lettuce is easy to grow, even in a patio garden. Sometimes you may stand there and eat it all before you get it in the house, but there is such satisfaction in creating a home cooked meal with veggies from your own garden!
As gardeners, we are aware of the need to take care of the environment that feeds us, body and soul. Planting trees, growing our own veggies, connecting with our family and paying attention to our global family is a thread we think most people embrace.
So really, every day can honor the earth – the cumulative effect of small efforts can indeed be great, can indeed make a difference.
Other earth-friendly ideas for your garden:
Start composting your yard debris. It makes a rich and wonderful soil amendment that you can cycle back into your garden – you can build a composting system or purchase a pre-made bin of wire or recycled plastic.
Make a worm bin. A worm bin makes a great family project and it's amazing to watch bits of vegetables, fruit peels and egg shells turn into rich material for your soil (or as a great addition to your compost pile). You can purchase a pre-made worm bin or make your own.
Disconnect your downspout and install a rain barrel. Your garden will thank you and you'll save some money on your utility bills.
Grow some of your own organic food. It's better for you, better for the earth. You don’t need a lot of ground, either; lots of vegetables and herbs grow happily in containers, and some even in shade.
Make room for native plants in your landscape. Natives need less supplemental water, attract native butterflies and other important pollinators, provide food and shelter for birds.
And while you're at it, plant a row for the hungry, or just plant a few extra veggies and take your excess harvest to a local food pantry for those who are food-insecure. You can contact the Oregon Food Bank to find out the food donation site closest to your garden.
