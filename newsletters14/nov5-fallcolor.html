Fall Planting tips from Laura

We have so many great deciduous perennials and grasses in our clearance area right now. Although they don’t look like much now, they will be great come spring!

Fall is the best time for planting because our winters are mild enough temperature wise, the rains keep things moist and plants can continue to grow roots most of the winter.

If you are planting cold hardy (that means plants hardy to USDA zones 7 and below) deciduous plants will be perfectly fine even if we have a hard winter and so will most cold hardy evergreen plants.

What you need to watch out for is the "borderline hardy" (plants rated to zone 8 or above) and things that need excellent drainage. This is because these plants will not have a root system established enough to withstand extremely cold temperatures and tolerate the copious amounts of rain we receive in the winter.

These are just basic guidelines for planting in the ground in unprotected areas. There are so many exceptions to the rules (of course it's gardening).

In summary, what I would plant now:

Hardy deciduous and evergreen grasses, perennials, ferns, shrubs trees and conifers.

Ground covers that are not picky about soil drainage such as Kinnickinick,
Rubus pentalous, Pachysandra, and Vinca.

Bulbs and garlic!

Ornamental cabbage and kale, pansies and dusty miller.

Things I wouldn't plant in the ground now but would fare through the winter in a sheltered location such as a covered porch include:

Cyclamen persica, large leaved Hebes, Phormiums, and plants that need good drainage such as creeping thyme.
