Earth Day is by now a standard tradition in most people’s lives. Having been around for forty-three years, there is an entire generation (or two) for whom the 22nd of April is as much a part of their annual calendar as any other holiday. People turn out in droves to mark the day to appreciate the natural world through acts of service and celebration.

This year is no exception: Plans are in the works for school and neighborhood cleanups, flower and tree plantings; ground breaking events for new school and community gardens; kids starting seeds in milk-carton containers at school to take home and plant in their own gardens. Every year Portland Nursery donates and discounts supplies for a variety of Earth Day events.

As gardeners, we especially are aware of the need to take care of the environment that feeds us, body and soul.

And really, every day can be Earth Day – the cumulative effect of small efforts can indeed be great, can indeed make a difference.

Here are some ideas to try for your own celebrating of Earth Day:

  • Start composting your yard debris – it makes a rich and wonderful soil amendment that you can cycle back into your garden – you can build a composting system or purchase a premade bin of wire or recycled plastic.
  • Even though we Portlanders can now also recycle our kitchen scraps, it's still fun to have a worm bin – makes a great family project, too – it's a sort of garden nerdy thing, but amazing to watch bits of vegetables, fruit peels and egg shells turn into rich material for your soil (or as a great addition to your compost pile). You can purchase a pre-made worm bin or make your own (Zenger Farm has a fun class for making your own worm bin - a fun, kid-friendly class!)
  • Disconnect your downspout and install a rain barrel - your garden will thank you and you'll save some money on your utility bills.
  • Grow some of your own organic food. It's better for you, better for the earth. You don’t need a lot of ground, either; lots of vegetables and herbs grow happily in containers, and some even in shade.
  • If you have the available space, why not plant a tree? (That way, you can celebrate Earth Day and Arbor Day at the same time.) Trees attract birds to your garden; provide shade that helps to cool your house and cut down on need for air-conditioning; and fruit trees have the added benefits of attracting pollinators to your yard and give you tasty fresh produce.
  • Make room for native plants in your landscape. Natives need less supplemental water, attract native butterflies and other important pollinators, provide food and shelter for birds.

And while you're at it, plant a row for the hungry, or just plant a few extra veggies and take your excess harvest to a local food pantry for those who are food-insecure. You can contact the Oregon Food Bank to find out the food donation site closest to your garden.
