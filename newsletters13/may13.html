DO YOU KNOW ABOUT OUR BUILD A CONTAINER PAGE?

May is a good time to be planting your containers and our Container Page has all you need to create spectacular splashes of color for your deck or patio.

Use the simple Thriller, Spiller, Filler formula: start with a “thriller,” a centerpiece plant with star quality, something big, bold, and beautiful like a red and green coleus. Surround it with a filler, for example, a fuchsia and then fill the remainder with a spiller such as Sedum 'Ogon.'

Use each of the plants—in various proportions—taking care to balance colors and textures. We have selections of a rainbow of colors in each. Refer to these lists to select plants for each:

  • New Arrivals for 2013
  • Thriller, Spillers, and Fillers for Shade
  • Thriller, Spillers, and Fillers for Sun

Rose Essentials

  • At least 6 hours of sun a day. Attempting roses with less sun is an uphill (and generally losing) battle.
  • Good air circulation is essential to keeping disease at bay. Plant roses at least 3 feet from other plants. Prune out branches that grow toward the inside of the plant.
  • Roses will perform best (lots of flowers & limited disease) if they are fertilized. Choose a fertilizer that is labeled for use on roses. Whether the food is organic or synthetic, liquid or granular, follow the directions on the package exactly.
  • Water in the morning so leaves can dry (on sunny days) before night-time. Use enough water to soak the roots completely, but do it just once a week for established plants. If temperatures are very hot, water more often.

Azalea Lace Bug, Drosophila and Downy Mildew - Oh My!

AZALEA LACE BUG

Azalea lace bug, Stephanitis pyrioides can turn evergreen azalea plants in landscapes nearly white from feeding damage. This lace bug is also causing damage on rhododendrons, the damage appears to be more severe than damage reported from rhododendron lace bug which has been in the PNW for some time and is reported to have only one generation per year compared to multiple generations reported for azalea lacebug. Mountain laurel is reported as an alternate host for azalea lace bug (Smith-Fiola).

Products containing neonicotinoid such as Bayer Tree and Shrub is one option for control. Natural products include Bonide pyrethrin concentrate, Monterey Takedown (pyrethrin AND canola oil: pyrethrin to kill and oil to smother eggs), Azamax (which is azadirachtin…basically a super charged neem oil). Monterey Neem Oil and Safer Insecticidal Soap round out our options.

This article from Oregon State University provides a good summary of the Azalea Lace Bug. You do not need to remove your affected plants; just treat them with one of the above methods, preferably now, before the Lace Bugs attack the new growth.

DROSOPHILA: SPOTTED WING FRUIT FLY (SWD)

We have been getting reports that Drosophila or SWD, spotted wing fruit fly or two spot fruit fly, has been hitting cane berries. This is another problem you need to stay on top of this year.

This article on Spotted Wing Drosophila has all of the information on controlling this pest.

Some of the products for lace bug can cross over for the Drosophila like Azamax. Anything with Spinosad is great as another natural option. Chemically it would be Malathion.
