Exciting new varieties for 2013

Every year plant breeders introduce some new plant varieties. Last year we saw previews at trade shows and trial crops. Here are some of the new introductions that stood out. They will be available at both our stores a soon as the crops are ready! (Shown left to right above)

Calibrachoa ‘Lemon Slice’

The cover model for the Proven Winners catalogue is this darling yellow and white striped Calibrachoa. Light yellow is a cheery neutral color that can combine with anything. For a refreshing sunny combination try Lemon Slice with light green sweet potato vine and white Calibrachoa.

Carex oshimensis ‘Everillo’

This new sedge features intense gold leaves that fade to chartreuse in summer. It thrives in full sun to part shade (color holds gold longer with afternoon shade). It has an elegant weeping, clump form reaching 1’ tall 2’ wide. It will look fantastic with Hostas and Heucheras! Oh and best of all, its evergreen! Hardy in zones 5-9.

Salvia ‘Amistad’

This new Salvia has us salivating! Royal purple flowers sit in black calyxes. Very similar to the adored Salvia ‘Black and Blue’ Amistad grows 3-4’ high and 3’ wide with full sun and good drainage. It is drought tolerant when established. Blooms appear summer through fall and attract hummingbirds and butterflies. The root is cold hardy to 18°F. The plant combinations for this beauty are endless!

Saruma henryi

We are so excited for the return of this wonderful, Chinese, woodland perennial. Velvety heart shaped leaves emerge in early spring, growing 18-24” tall. Darling three-petaled butter yellow flowers bloom April and May. Related to wild ginger, this perennial thrives in full to part shade, well-drained soil, regular moisture in zones 5-8.

Chenopodium capitatum Beetberry/Strawberry Spinach

A wonderful, low maintenance, edible plant for your sustainable food garden! Centuries old heirloom. Native to most of North America, also found in parts of Europe and New Zealand. Primarily grown for its spinach-green, triangular leaves, Beetberry can be eaten raw or cooked. Both the leaves and berries can be used in salad. Berries are low acid/pleasantly sweet, with a beet-like flavor. As always - all things in moderation! Don’t overdo it with the raw salads. It does have oxalic acid which can irritate those with arthritis, etc. Cooking will reduce oxalic acid content.

Tolerates heat well and is often used as a summertime spinach-replacement, w/long harvest time.

18”H x 12 “W. Needs no staking or supports.

 

What to pay attention to in March? (Critters)

New Approaches to Old Problems.

Slugs and Snails

You are not the only one discovering all those wonderful bulbs popping up everywhere. Slugs and snails are preparing to dine so this would be the time to bait. Options range from the old fashioned beer trap (Colorado State funded a research project that found non-alcoholic beer was the most effective!), to chemical and natural baits. There is a major movement by the traditional slug bait companies to now move away from the chemical ingredient Metaldehyde as they have seen consumers want a safer alternative. Corry’s for example has made the big switch and now offers slug bait containing Sodium Ferric EDTA which basically is iron combined with sodium. Safe to use and very effective.

Cut Worms

Cut worms are starting to appear this month as the soil temps rise. The main way to decide if you have cutworm or slug damage is if the plants are mowed down liie someone stomped through your garden at night, it’s probably cutworm. Go out at night with a flashlight to check as most of the damage is nocturnal. Beneficial Nematodes can help with the problem although soil temp is restrictive. Above 49°F is optimal. Another natural spray would be anything that contains Spinosad which is a bacteria originally discovered in the soil of an abandoned rum distillery.

Mildew

Disease wise we are starting to hear talk of mildew showing up. Diseases like mildew are common here and methods of control are varied. The biggest thing you can do to swing the balance in your favor is to catch the problem early. Mildew is characterized by fluffy white or powdery white material on the leaf surface. Impatiens get it, roses get it, most plants can get it. You can do everything right and still get it. Systemic (taken up by the plant) methods are great preventatives. Topical sprays include Daconil, Neem Oils, Copper and Serenade. Serenade is wonderful not just because it works, but it is one of the only products I know of that will not harm bees.

Diligence is an important part of dealing with pest and disease problems in your garden. The sooner the better, but realize that a completely sterile environment is not only impossible but unhealthy as well in that you need to encourage a balance.
