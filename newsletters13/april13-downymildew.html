Tomato Varieties

Did You know we carry 334 varieties of tomatoes?!

This does not even include grafted tomatoes. Of course, it is impossible for us to have all of these varieties at one time, and during the season we rotate in different varieties.

We attempt to keep the classics like Beefsteak, Early Girl, Brandywine, Roma, San Marzano, Sun Gold, Sweet 100 and Sweet Million on our shelves. Plus there are a number of Heirlooms that we regularly carry. More unusual varieties like Applause, Green Giant, Odoriko, Russian 117, we may only get in once.

Our peak inventory for tomatoes is the last week of April through the middle of May. We will still carry tomatoes into June, but the selection decreases.

If you have one certain variety in mind, it may pay to have a backup choice when you come into the store. We are bound to have some exciting substitute that you may just come to love. Another strategy, that many of our customers use, is to visit us several times during tomato season until their collection is complete. Usually all of our weekly tomato orders are here by Thursday or Friday. Use our buyer’s lists to pick out a few varieties you think would be best in your garden.

Know before you grow…Impatiens Downy Mildew

Impatiens Downy Mildew

Our beloved bedding Impatiens have a new threat in the Pacific Northwest: Impatiens Downy Mildew is a deadly fungal disease which thrives in moist, humid conditions. It specifically infects Impatiens walleriana and hybrids, bedding Impatiens (6 pack style), Rosebud Impatiens, Butterfly and Fusion Impatiens.

SYMPTOMS

  • Early signs include:
    • Light green yellowing or stippling of leaves
    • Leaf edges curl down
    • Faint gray streaks on upper leaf surface
    • White cotton-like patches on undersides of leaves
  • Advanced signs include:
    • Stunted plants
    • Leaf and flower drop, leaving bare stems (which occurs rapidly)
    • Infected stems collapse in cool wet conditions

CONTROL

The spores of this disease spread by wind and water (rain and overhead irrigation). One source says the spores can spread hundreds of miles on the wind! Also if infected stems are left to rot on the soil the spores can persist in the soil for two to three years.

Once you have the disease there is no control. DO NOT COMPOST INFECTED PLANTS! Please put them in a plastic bag as close to the area they were growing in as possible, and put them in the garbage.

DO NOT PLANT MORE IMPATIENS IN THE INFECTED AREA. They will die. It is best to not plant Impatiens walleriana in an infected area for at least three years.

Growers can prevent this disease with clean facilities, regular monitoring and weekly applications of fungicides that are mostly not available to the home grower.

Actinovate is an organic fungicide Portland Nursery carries, that can prevent infection and control the disease. Visit the Actinovate website for more details.

Alternatives to Impatiens walleriana

 

Impatiens walleriana at Portland Nursery

At this time we are planning to carry all types of Impatiens walleriana this year, but in smaller quantities. Our growers have taken measures to ensure we can offer you healthy plants. We will also post warnings near the plants to notify customers of the potential threat and suggest alternatives.

As always, Portland Nursery is invested in your success in the garden, and we strive to provide you with all the information and tools you need to be so.

Growing alternatives to Impatiens walleriana (listed below) is another option.

ALTERNATIVES

There are many colorful alternatives for the shade garden:

  • Alyssum (part shade)
  • Bacopa (part shade)
  • Begonias all types
  • Coleus
  • Dichondra (part sun)
  • Euphorbia ‘Diamond Frost’ and ‘Breathless Blush’
  • Fuchsias
  • Helitrope (part shade)
  • Hypoestes (polka dot plant)
  • Lobelia (part shade)
  • New Guinea Impatiens (they are a different species!)
  • Strobilanthes
  • Sweet Potato vine
  • Torenia (Wishbone Flower)
