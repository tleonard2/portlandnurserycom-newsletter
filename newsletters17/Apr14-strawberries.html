Tips for Planting Strawberries
When you plant your strawberries, space them 12-15” apart in rows. To allow space for runners, plant 18-24” apart with 3-4 feet between rows. Be careful to plant with crowns just above ground level. Remove flowers at planting. You can also use strawberry pots or hanging baskets to hold your berry plants.
Maintaining Your Strawberry Plants
Strawberries need full sun and soil that drains well. Use raised mounds when planting in heavy soil. Keep well watered throughout season (about one inch of water per week); mulch to conserve moisture. Hand weed your berries because they are shallow rooted and do not compete well. Fertilize twice a year, once in spring and again after first crop with a fertilizer high in phosphorus. Replace plants every two to three years with new varieties or runners produced from your existing plants. One plant produces about one quart of berries.
Strawberries are divided into 2 classes by fruit bearing habit:
JUNE-BEARING: Produces one heavy crop in late spring/early summer. Excellent flavor, preferred for preserves or freezing since they produce heavy quantities at one time.
DAY NEUTRAL/EVERBEARING: Day neutral means that the plant is not affected by the length of the day. Produces fruit summer through fall, but not heavily at any one time. Flavor is excellent; great for eating fresh. These are best for container plantings.
Albion - Everbearing. An outstanding variety from the University of California, Albion combines superior flavor with good resistance to verticillium wilt and crown rots. Delicious and large berries have intense red coloring all the way through, this high yielding strawberry is sure to delight! Please note, these send out runners.
Quinalt - Everbearing. Very large, tasty, soft berries. Good for fresh eating and desserts. Vigorous grower with high yield and a long harvest season. Resistant to root rot and leaf spot, but susceptible to mildew. Please note, these send out runners. Great for containers.
Seascape - Everbearing. A Portland Nursery staff favorite! Large, firm berry with outstanding, very sweet flavor and excellent color. A reliable producer with a long harvest season. Wonderful for fresh eating and disease resistant.
Hood - June bearing. A Northwest favorite with very large, glossy dark red fruit. High quality, sweet flavor. Excellent for processing and good for other uses. Very productive and upright plant makes for easily picked berries. Resistant to root rot and mildew.
Tristar - Everbearing. Medium size, deep red berries with medium red flesh. Excellent flavor, good for fresh eating and freezing. Produces a very early crop and heavy fall crop. Recommended for hanging baskets.

Lonicera: Honeysuckle Vine
Honeysuckle flowers are often tubular forming a deep throat, attracting birds and bees. Seems like everyone grew up with at least one honeysuckle vine in their yard; the fragrance is captivating for us and for the hummingbirds. Around 180 species of deciduous or evergreen shrubs and vines make up the Honeysuckle genus.
Honeysuckles are very adaptable, enjoying many soil types and varying pH with the exception being very wet boggy soil. They thrive in full sun to part shade. Hardiness varies depending on species.
Prune honeysuckles directly after flowering. Vines will need training during their rapid growth phase and (at least) yearly pruning to keep them where you want them. If plants become overgrown and gangly they can be pruned hard to the ground and they will grow back with multiple shoots.
