Forty-two years after it began, Earth Day is still celebrated and is stronger than ever! Plans are in the works for school cleanups and tree plantings; ground breaking events for new school and community gardens; kids starting seeds in milk-carton containers at school to take home and plant in their own gardens; lots of volunteer opportunities for neighborhood cleanup/planting/restoration projects throughout the city.

Every year we donate and discount supplies for a variety of Earth Day events. This year's recipients include: Oregon College of Arts and Crafts, Hacienda CDC, Richmond Elementary School, Llewellyn Elementary School, Creston Elementary School, Pleasant Valley School, Harrison Park School and Glencoe Elementary School.

As gardeners, we especially are aware of the need to take care of the environment that feeds us, body and soul.

While it has become a cliché that "every day is Earth Day," it can also be true! We can do a little bit, all the time, to make this ground we live on just a little bit better.

Here are some ideas to try for your own celebrating of Earth Day:

  • Start composting your yard debris – it makes a rich and wonderful soil amendment that you can cycle back into your garden – you can build a composting system or purchase a premade bin of wire or recycled plastic.
  • Even though we Portlanders can now also recycle our kitchen scraps, it's still fun to have a worm bin – makes a great family project, too – it's a sort of garden nerdy thing, but amazing to watch bits of vegetables, fruit peels and egg shells turn into rich material for your soil (or as a great addition to your compost pile).
  • Disconnect your downspout and install a rain barrel - your garden will thank you and you'll save some money on your utility bills.
  • Grow your own food, organically. It's better for you, better for the earth.
  • If you have the available space, why not plant a tree? (That way, you can celebrate Earth Day and Arbor Day at the same time.) Trees attract birds to your garden; provide shade that helps to cool your house and cut down on need for air-conditioning; and fruit trees have the added benefits of attracting pollinators to your yard and give you tasty fresh produce.
  • Make room for native plants in your landscape. Natives need less supplemental water, attract native butterflies and other important pollinators, often provide food and shelter for birds.

And while you're at it, plant a row for the hungry, or just plant a few extra veggies and take your excess harvest to a local food pantry for those who are food-insecure. You can contact the Oregon Food Bank to find out the food donation site closest to your garden.
